Muhammad sends a man to kill the stepson who married his father's wife — and to seize his wealth Incest Hudud Prophetic Character Governance Strong Nasa'i #3337, #3338
"I met my maternal uncle who was carrying a flag (for an expedition) and I said: 'Where are you going?' He said: 'The Messenger of Allah is sending me to a man who has married his father's wife after he died, to strike his neck or kill him.'" Parallel chain (#3338): "...and he has commanded me to strike his neck (kill him) and seize his wealth."

What the hadith says

Al-Bara' ibn 'Azib narrates an incident from a Prophetic-era expedition: his maternal uncle is dispatched, banner in hand, on a mission to execute a specific man. The man's offense is that he married his late father's widow — i.e., his stepmother. The kill order in #3338 is appended with a wealth-seizure mandate: not just execution, but property confiscation. Both chains are graded sound; the hadith is preserved in parallel by Abu Dawud and Ibn Majah.

Why this is a problem

  1. The penalty is military execution by Prophetic decree. This is not a courtroom hadd applied after evidence; it is a military expedition dispatched specifically to kill a single named man for a private domestic act. The Prophet's banner-and-spearman procedure is preserved as the model for handling certain offenses.
  2. "Strike his neck and seize his wealth" combines hudud and confiscation. Classical jurisprudence treats hadd executions as lethal but property-respecting (the executed man's heirs inherit). The Prophetic order in #3338 overrides that protection: the wealth goes to the state. The implicit theology is that the offender forfeits not just life but lineage's legal economic continuity.
  3. The "incest" categorization assumes the marriage was consensual. The hadith does not record any allegation that the man coerced his stepmother. The marriage as such — to a woman who had been his father's wife — is the offense. Q 4:22 forbids marriage to one's father's wife as a "shameful and hateful" act. The hadith adds the death penalty.
  4. It opens the door to private domestic relations as state-execution matters. If marrying one's stepmother warrants military assassination, the Prophet's authority over private domestic life extends to lethal sanction. The fiqh that follows (Hanbali Ahmad ibn Hanbal explicitly: "whoever marries his mother is killed"; Tirmidhi #1484 confirms the rule: "whoever has relations with mahram, kill him") inherits this scope.
  5. Modern Muslim states retain the structural pattern. Saudi Arabia's apostasy and adultery laws, Iran's "moral crimes" enforcement, the Taliban's morality-police executions — each operates on the principle that private domestic-and-sexual conduct can warrant state lethal sanction. The hadith provides the prophetic template.

Philosophical polemic: a religion whose prophetic record includes a banner-led expedition to assassinate a man for marrying his late father's wife has institutionalised state lethal authority over private domestic life. The "seize his wealth" addendum makes the punishment economic-political as well as biological. This is not the religion of conscience the modern apologetic projects; it is the religion of public sanction that classical jurisprudence inherited and ran on for centuries. Modern reform consists precisely in not running on it. That reform is welcome — but it is a departure from the canonical hadith, not its application.

The Muslim response

Classical apologetics frames the killing as the standard Hadd for the most serious incest case (a man marrying his late father's actual widow rather than just having an extra-marital relation), and the wealth seizure as standard treatment of a person executed for capital crime. Modern Muslim teaching often reads the hadith as historical context: the early Madinan polity was establishing baseline kinship taboos against pre-Islamic practice (including widow-inheritance, which Q 4:19 abolishes), and the dramatic enforcement was calibrated to that transitional moment.

Why it fails

The "transitional baseline" reading concedes the descriptive point: dramatic state lethal violence against private domestic conduct was the operating method. The "transitional" framing implies the method was time-bound; classical jurisprudence did not treat it that way. The schools generalised the principle ("kill the one who marries mahram") rather than time-boxing it. Modern Muslim states that no longer execute stepson-stepmother marriages have reformed away from the canonical hadith; they have not implemented its current meaning. The "historical context" defense is the apologist's escape, not the legal hermeneutic that operated for fourteen centuries.

"Any man who tries to create division among my Ummah, strike his neck" — Nasa'i preserves the anti-dissent kill-order Apostasy & Blasphemy Governance Hudud Strong Nasa'i #4033
"Any man who goes out and tries to create division among my Ummah, strike his neck (kill him)."

What the hadith says

Usamah bin Sharik narrates Muhammad's blunt directive: anyone who attempts to fracture the Muslim community is to be killed. Nasa'i places the hadith in his Book on Fighting (Kitab Tahrim al-Damm) — the same book that preserves the seven-fold "whoever changes his religion, kill him" cluster. The juridical environment is consistent: dissent and apostasy are merged into a single capital-offense category.

Why this is a problem

  1. "Tries to create division" is an open-ended qualifier. Founding a new madhhab? Disagreeing with the caliph? Preaching a sectarian variant? Forming a political opposition? The hadith provides no procedural threshold, no evidentiary standard, no opportunity to repent. The trigger is the act of "going out" with divisive intent.
  2. It is the canonical scriptural backbone of the Sunni anti-Khariji legal regime. Classical jurisprudence cites this hadith (and parallels in Muslim, Abu Dawud) when justifying state execution of breakaway groups. The Khawarij, the Mu'tazilah's political wings, the early Shi'i revolts, the Qarmatians, and more recently the Ahmadiyya in Pakistan have all been prosecuted under variants of this rule.
  3. Combined with #4067 (three permissible-blood cases), it makes "splitting the Ummah" effectively a fourth case. The earlier hadith fixes capital offenses at three (post-marriage adultery, murder, apostasy). This hadith opens a fourth. Classical Sunni jurisprudence has often resolved the tension by reading "splitting the Ummah" as a sub-case of apostasy — which only widens apostasy's scope.
  4. The Sunni-Shia split satisfies the criterion on both sides. By the hadith's own logic, both Sunnis and Shi'is have been "creating division among my Ummah" since the seventh century. Each side has periodically applied the kill-order to the other (the Mongol-era Hanbali fatwas, the Saudi Wahhabi anti-Shi'i campaigns, the Iran-Iraq war's mutual takfir). The hadith does not specify which division-making warrants execution; it has functioned as a permission slip for whichever side has political power.
  5. It is structurally incompatible with religious freedom. If "creating division" is capital, no minority can lawfully preach reform. Ahmadiyya Muslims in Pakistan are constitutionally classified as non-Muslim (1974) and prosecuted under blasphemy law; the Tirmidhi-Nasa'i kill-Ummah-divider hadiths form part of the doctrinal scaffolding for that legal arrangement. Modern Muslim states that protect religious freedom do so against the canonical hadith.

Philosophical polemic: a religion whose canonical hadith corpus authorises the execution of anyone "trying to create division" has produced a tradition in which any reformist preaching is, in principle, a capital offense. The hadith does not require violence on the dissenter's part; mere advocacy of difference is the trigger. Fourteen centuries of intra-Muslim sectarian repression — Sunni against Khariji, Sunni against Shi'i, Sunni against Mu'tazili, Sunni against Wahhabi (and Wahhabi against everyone else) — have run on this canonical structure. The structure is not a misreading of the texts; the texts encode it.

The Muslim response

Classical apologetics frames the hadith as a narrow rule against armed rebellion against the legitimate caliph: "creating division" means physical insurrection, not theological dissent. The hadith is read as the Islamic equivalent of treason law — capital because the early-community survival required unity against existential military threats. Modern Muslim teaching adds that contemporary contexts allow far broader theological pluralism within the umma without triggering the rule.

Why it fails

The "armed rebellion only" reading is selectively applied. Classical jurisprudence consistently classified theological dissent as "splitting the Ummah" — Mu'tazilah, Khawarij, Mu'attila, Ash'ariyyah, Maturidi, Shi'a, Sufi orders have all been periodically denounced under this rubric, often with no insurrectionist component. The "narrow" reading is the modern apologetic; the classical reading was open-ended. Pakistan's 1974 constitutional declaration of Ahmadi non-Muslim status, Saudi religious-police executions, Iran's prosecution of Baha'i — each shows the rule operating against non-violent religious difference, with full classical-Sunni scriptural cover. The reform reading is improvement; it is not implementation.

"It is not permissible to shed the blood of a Muslim except in three cases" — Nasa'i preserves the apostasy clause Apostasy & Blasphemy Hudud Prophetic Character Strong Nasa'i #4067
"It is not permissible to shed the blood of a Muslim except in three cases: A man who commits adultery after having married; or one who kills another person, who is to be killed; or who reverts to Kufr after having accepted Islam, who is to be killed."

What the hadith says

Uthman ibn Affan, the third Caliph, narrates that Muhammad named exactly three offenses for which a Muslim's life is forfeit: post-marriage adultery, murder, and apostasy. The hadith fixes apostasy as a capital offense in the same breath as murder.

Why this is a problem

  1. Apostasy is grouped with murder. The triplet is not "murder, treason, brigandage." It is "adultery, murder, apostasy." Religious change is moralised at the level of taking a life.
  2. It is narrated by a Caliph. Uthman is not a marginal narrator. The hadith carries the political authority of the early caliphate, which makes it unusually load-bearing in classical jurisprudence on apostasy.
  3. "Reverts to Kufr" applies to the act, not a process. The hadith does not require persistent public preaching, treason, or organised revolt. The bare act of leaving Islam is the trigger.
  4. It is reinforced by parallel "Whoever changes his religion" reports. Nasa'i preserves seven separate chains of "kill him" (#4068–#4072) on the same page-cluster. The triple-redundancy makes the rule legally hard to dismiss.
  5. 13 Muslim-majority jurisdictions retain apostasy law. Saudi Arabia, Iran, Mauritania, Sudan, Yemen, Qatar, parts of Malaysia and Nigeria, and the Maldives — each draws theological cover from the canonical apostasy hadiths Nasa'i preserves.

Philosophical polemic: a religion that lists apostasy alongside murder as one of the three ways a believer's life can be lawfully ended is not a religion of voluntary conscience. The "no compulsion in religion" verse (2:256) cannot govern the same legal system as this hadith without contradiction; classical jurisprudence resolved that tension in favour of the hadith, not the verse.

The Muslim response

Classical apologetics argues "reverting to Kufr" historically meant active treason against the Muslim polity — a public renunciation that joined the enemy in wartime. Modern interpretations distinguish private apostasy (no penalty) from political apostasy (treason). The hadith, on this reading, criminalises sedition, not conscience.

Why it fails

The "treason only" reading is a modern editorial overlay. The hadith's wording is bare: the man "reverts to Kufr after having accepted Islam." There is no "and joins the enemy," no "and bears arms," no "and incites public revolt." Classical jurists across all four Sunni schools — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali — read the hadith literally and codified death for ordinary apostasy with no treason requirement. The treason-gloss appears in 20th-century apologetic literature, not in the texts the hadith generated. The "13 jurisdictions" figure is the operational evidence: this is law, not historical curiosity.

The Uraniyyin — eyes branded with heated nails, hands and feet cut off, left to die of thirst Prophetic Character Treatment of Disbelievers Strong Nasa'i #306, #307
"Their eyes were smoldered with heated nails, their hands and feet cut off, then they were left in Al-Harrah in that state until they died."

What the hadith says

A tribal group converted, drank camel urine and milk as medical prescription, recovered, apostatized, killed the Muslim herdsman. Muhammad's sentence: heated iron nails pressed into their eyes to blind them, hands and feet amputated on opposite sides, left in the volcanic black rocks without water until they died.

Why this is a problem

  1. The blinding is additional to the amputation and death. Not one punishment but three stacked.
  2. Water was denied deliberately. The dying-of-thirst was the mechanism of death. The victims begged for water; it was refused by Muhammad's order.
  3. Nasa'i's commentary calls this "just retribution." The tradition's own apologetic defends the torture as proportionate.
  4. Modern international humanitarian law would classify this as war crimes. The defining features — blinding, mutilation, denial of water to a dying captive — are textbook examples.

Philosophical polemic: a prophet whose signature punishment combines blinding with iron, amputation, and engineered death by thirst is a prophet whose biography includes acts that no universal ethical framework can accommodate. Nasa'i's preservation and defense of the episode is the problem — not just the original incident.

"Drink camel urine and milk" — Nasa'i's version of prophetic medicine Science Claims Strange / Obscure Moderate Nasa'i #306
"The Messenger ordered them to go to the camels and drink their urine and milk."

What the hadith says

Muhammad prescribed camel urine as medicine to cure climate-illness. Nasa'i's commentary derives that the urine of meat-halal animals is ritually pure — and recommends the practice.

Why this is a problem

  1. Drinking urine is medically harmful. Urine contains nitrogenous waste products. Re-ingesting them stresses kidneys and risks infection.
  2. WHO warns against during MERS-CoV outbreaks. MERS is zoonotically linked to camels.
  3. Cross-collection preservation. Bukhari, Muslim, Tirmidhi, Nasa'i all have versions.
  4. "Prophetic medicine" markets still sell camel urine products.

Philosophical polemic: a revelation whose medical prescription is drinking another species' urine is a revelation whose medical content fails modern medicine — and has caused harm to Muslims who trusted it.

The Muslim response

Apologists argue the hadith records a specific therapeutic prescription for a group of men suffering from a particular Medinan illness — an ad hoc treatment using what was available in the 7th-century Arabian environment, not a standing medical endorsement of urine-drinking. Some modern apologists further cite studies on camel urine's alkaline composition as potentially bactericidal, framing the hadith as unwittingly pointing at a pharmacological property.

Why it fails

The "specific therapeutic" framing does not address the text's presentation of the Prophet as the prescriber. A divinely-informed prophet prescribing medical treatment should not be administering a remedy whose active effect is either nonexistent or drastically outweighed by its pathogen load — camel urine has been specifically implicated by the WHO as a vector for MERS-CoV transmission. The "modern studies on alkalinity" is retrofit: the hadith makes a concrete medical recommendation that modern epidemiology has specifically warned against. A revelation that would not need to be subsequently overruled by public-health institutions is precisely what a scripture-status medical claim should produce.
