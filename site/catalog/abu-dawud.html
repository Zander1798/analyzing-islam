"He who copies any people is one of them" — the tashabbuh cultural quarantine hadith Disbelievers Governance Moral Problems Internal Contradictions Logical Inconsistency Ritual Absurdities Strong Abu Dawud #4032
"The Messenger of Allah said: man tashabbaha bi-qawmin fa-huwa minhum — He who copies any people is one of them."

What the hadith says

Deliberate cultural imitation makes you a member of the imitated group. Ibn Taymiyyah built this into a comprehensive system prohibiting Muslims from imitating non-Muslims in clothing, festivals, and cultural practice. Modern Salafi fatawa deploy it against Christmas, neckties, birthday cakes, and specific hairstyles.

Why this is a problem

  1. Outward imitation becomes soteriology. A Muslim wearing a Christmas sweater has, on the plain reading, become "one of" the Christians — with the eternal consequences that carries. No limiting principle specifies which group or which degree of resemblance triggers the rule.
  2. It directly conflicts with Q 49:13 — "We made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another." The hadith's quarantine principle makes the mutual acquaintance that verse commands structurally impossible.

Why it fails

The religious-vs-cultural distinction is not in the hadith — it is a post-hoc juristic restriction applied to an unqualified statement. Ibn Taymiyyah's extension to culturally-neutral forms shows the plain text supports the broader application. Operational results — Saudi religious police, Taliban dress-regulation, widespread scrupulosity around cultural participation — are not misreadings; they are straightforward applications of what the text says.

What the hadith says

Abu Dawud #2769: Muhammad commissions the assassination of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf — a Jewish poet who insulted him — and explicitly permits the assassin to lie about Muhammad to gain Ka'b's trust. Abu Dawud #2770, one hadith later in the same book: "A believer should not assassinate."

Why this is a problem

  1. Muhammad pre-authorised deception — including slander of himself — as an assassination method. Classical commentary confirmed the permission as a war-necessity exception, setting a precedent cited from the Rushdie fatwa to the Charlie Hebdo murders.
  2. Abu Dawud placed an absolute prohibition against assassination one hadith after a concrete commission — an unresolved canonical self-contradiction preserved in the same chapter.

Why it fails

The canonical charge against Ka'b is that he "caused trouble" through speech and poetry — not armed conspiracy. If that makes him a legitimate assassination target, the category covers every critic and satirist — exactly how the precedent has been used. The planned operation with scripted lies is not an emergency requiring ad hoc deception. Abu Dawud preserved both hadiths knowing the tension; the tradition resolved it by applying the commission as operative sunnah while treating the prohibition as carrying Ka'b-based exceptions.

"Kill those who change their religion" — Abu Dawud's unconditional death sentence for apostasy Apostasy & Blasphemy Moral Problems Governance Hudud Internal Contradictions Strong Abu Dawud #4353
"'Ali burned some people who retreated from Islam... Ibn 'Abbas said: 'I would have killed them on account of the statement of the Messenger of Allah: Kill those who change their religion (man baddala dinahu faqtuluhu).'"

"Mu'adh said: I will not sit until he is killed according to the decision of Allah and His Apostle. He said it three times. He then commanded for it and he was killed." (#4356)

What the hadith says

Abu Dawud Book 40 establishes death as the canonical penalty for apostasy. The anchor text — "man baddala dinahu faqtuluhu" — is unconditional. The Yemen case law at #4356 shows Muʿadh executing a man for religious reversion alone, with no armed rebellion alleged.

Why this is a problem

  1. The command is unconditional — universal subject, no qualifier, imperative result. The Yemen case confirms: the executed man's only stated offense was religious reversion. Both ʿAli and Ibn ʿAbbas treated execution as mandatory.
  2. The ruling is active state law today. Saudi Arabia, Iran, Afghanistan, Mauritania, and others apply death or severe punishment for apostasy, with this hadith as the jurisprudential foundation.

Why it fails

The rebellion limitation is not in the canonical text, and the Yemen case law delivers a decisive counter-example: immediate execution for religious reversion alone. The reformist Quranic-primacy argument is the most honest position, but it requires prioritising Q 2:256 over a sahih hadith across all six canonical Sunni collections — a methodological move classical usul al-fiqh explicitly prohibits. Modern Muslim moral progress on apostasy requires overriding a direct Prophetic dictum. That is the honest statement of the problem.

"Whoever acquires knowledge of astrology acquires a branch of magic" Science Internal Contradictions Strange Theology Logic Strong Abu Dawud #3906
"The Prophet said: 'If anyone acquires any knowledge of astrology, he acquires a branch of magic of which he gets more as long as he continues to do so.'"

What the hadith says

Muhammad classifies 'ilm al-nujum — star knowledge, covering both astrology and astronomy in classical Arabic — as sihr (magic/sorcery). The more one studies, the more magical the contamination becomes.

Why this is a problem

  1. The Islamic Golden Age of astronomy thrived under religious patronage while this hadith was canonical. Al-Battani, al-Tusi, and Ibn al-Shatir produced major astronomical work in direct structural tension with the hadith's blanket condemnation.
  2. Jurists had to invent the astrology-vs-astronomy distinction post-Prophetically, because the hadith condemns all 'ilm al-nujum without qualification — yet Islamic prayer-times and qibla calculation required star-knowledge.

Why it fails

The distinction is post-Prophetic jurisprudential development; the canonical hadith classifies all star-knowledge as magic without it. Classical Islamic astronomers had to defend their work against accusations of forbidden astrology — which shows the distinction was never stable. Modern Saudi opposition to mathematical astronomy in moon-sighting contexts confirms the hadith's plain reading remains live. The text says stellar knowledge is a branch of magic; the tradition's celebrated scientific achievements required arguing around it.

"The Messenger of Allah said: 'There is no infection...' A nomadic Arab asked: 'How is it that when a mangy camel comes among healthy camels it gives them mange?' He replied: 'Who infected the first one?'

What the hadith says

Muhammad denies contagion as superstition. When a Bedouin pushes back with the observable fact of mangy camels infecting healthy ones, Muhammad deflects with a counter-question. The same chain preserves a contradicting Prophetic ruling — isolate diseased camels from healthy ones — which Abu Hurairah denied transmitting when faced with the contradiction.

Why this is a problem

  1. The "no contagion" claim is empirically false, and the Bedouin's observation is correct. Muhammad's counter-question redirects to ultimate causation without addressing the Bedouin's point about proximate transmission.
  2. The isolation ruling contradicts the denial — if contagion doesn't exist, why isolate diseased animals? Both cannot be simultaneously rational, and the tradition preserved both without resolution.

Why it fails

Muhammad's response to the Bedouin's observation was a deflecting counter-question, not "yes they transmit it, but Allah is the ultimate cause." The isolation rule is then silent evidence that Muhammad accepted contagion-prudence in practice — making the no-contagion declaration inexplicable. Abu Hurairah denying his own transmission is the community's own recognition that the contradiction was visible. The Bedouin was right; the plain text says Muhammad denied it.
