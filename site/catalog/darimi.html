Sunan al-Dārimī

Compiled by 'Abd Allāh al-Dārimī (d. 869 CE). Arranged thematically like a sunan, covering jurisprudence, worship, commerce, and the virtues of the Qurʾan. Approximately 3,400 hadiths across 23 books.

The Quran will be taken away — from hearts and from manuscripts Scripture Integrity Eschatology Strong Darimi 3246
"Increase in reciting the Qur'an before it is taken away. They said: These manuscripts (masahif) are taken away, but what about what is in the hearts of men? He said: It will be taken away at night, so they will wake up from it destitute, and they will forget the saying 'There is no god but Allah,' and will fall into the words of ignorance and their poetry, and that is when the word will come upon them."

What the hadith says

Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, one of the companions the Prophet endorsed as a Quran teacher, reports a warning that at the end times the Quran will be removed — first from written copies, then from human memory. People will wake up one morning having forgotten even the declaration of faith.

Why this is a problem

Quran 15:9 promises: "Indeed, it is We who sent down the Qur'an and indeed, We will be its guardian." The standard Islamic preservation doctrine reads this as divine guarantee that the Quran will never be lost or altered. But this sahih-grade hadith, transmitted from a companion the Prophet himself appointed as a Quran teacher, directly states that the Quran will be taken away — both from manuscripts and from hearts. Either 15:9's promise has a temporal limit the text does not specify, or the hadith contradicts the preservation promise outright.

The Muslim response

Classical apologetics treats the hadith as eschatological — the removal occurs at the very end of time, as a sign of the Hour rather than a failure of preservation during the era of faith. Allah preserves the Quran for as long as there is a community of believers; the removal only happens when belief itself has already effectively collapsed. The hadith describes the end of the age of preservation, not its interruption within it.

Why it fails

The apologetic rescue requires reading an unstated temporal limitation into Quran 15:9 — the verse promises preservation without qualification, and the hadith describes loss without qualification. Either the promise is unconditional (and the hadith contradicts it) or the promise has hidden conditions (and the claim of unconditional divine preservation is rhetorical rather than literal). The specific detail — loss from both masahif (material copies) and human memory — forecloses the usual defense that "the Quran is preserved in memory even if no physical copy survived." The hadith explicitly names both modes of preservation and asserts both fail. A scripture cannot both be guaranteed against textual loss and describe its own textual loss without one of the claims being rhetorical.

"Water from water" — the ablution rule abrogated within the Prophet's lifetime Contradictions Scripture Integrity Moderate Darimi Book 1 (Purification) ~753–755
"The water is from the water" — i.e., ghusl (full ritual bath) is required only where there is seminal emission.

What the hadith says

Sunan al-Dārimī preserves, in its Book of Purification, the earlier prophetic ruling that full ablution was required only on emission (al-mā' min al-mā' — "the water is from the water"). The tradition also preserves the subsequent abrogation of this rule: ghusl came to be required on sexual contact regardless of emission.

Why this is a problem

An essential rule of ritual purity — one that governs every Muslim's daily prayer life — was issued by the Prophet, practiced by the early community, and then overturned within the same lifetime. If the earlier rule was divinely authorised, Allah rescinded it. If it was not divinely authorised, the Prophet's ritual legislation was corrigible human reasoning, not revelation. Either way, Islam's claim that prophetic Sunnah is a reliable extension of divine guidance has a real problem at the level of its most routine ritual.

The Muslim response

Classical jurisprudence treats intra-lifetime abrogation as pedagogical — Allah legislates progressively to train the community, with the earlier ruling serving as a stepping-stone rather than an error. The full-ablution-on-contact rule represents a clarification, not a correction, and reflects Allah's gradual elevation of the community's ritual standards. The preserved earlier rule is evidence of transparent transmission, not of theological instability.

Why it fails

"Pedagogical progression" is the standard apologetic framing for any abrogated rule, and it faces the same structural problem whenever invoked: if the earlier rule was the right rule for its time, then ritual correctness is time-dependent (which cuts against Islam's claim to timeless moral-ritual truth); if it was never the right rule, Allah caused a false practice to be taught under divine authority. The specific case here is awkward because the subject is ritual purity — not a complex moral matter where gradualism might be expected, but a basic physical practice. The hadith's own term naskh (abrogation) is in play, which is exactly the doctrinal category classical Islamic scholars invoked to explain it. What the tradition preserves is the evidence that ritual law was not stable even within Muhammad's own lifetime.
